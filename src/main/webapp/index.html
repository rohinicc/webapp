What is a Nebula?

An interstellar cloud of gas, dust, hydrogen, helium and other ionised plasma — the birthplace of stars and the graveyard of ancient suns.

🌌
Stellar Nurseries
Emission nebulae like the Orion Nebula are active star-forming regions where gravity collapses dense molecular clouds into protostars over millions of years.
Formation
💫
Cosmic Scale
Most nebulae span tens to hundreds of light-years across. The Tarantula Nebula in the Large Magellanic Cloud stretches over 1,000 light-years wide.
Scale
🔭
Light & Colour
The vivid colours arise from ionised gases: hydrogen glows red and pink, oxygen emits teal and blue-green, sulfur produces oranges and reds.
Spectroscopy
⚡
Dark Nebulae
Dense dust clouds so thick they block background starlight entirely. The Horsehead Nebula is a famous dark nebula silhouetted against a glowing emission region.
Opacity
🌠
Planetary Nebulae
When a sun-like star exhausts its fuel, it sheds its outer layers into a glowing shell. These stunning rings mark the final chapter of a star's life.
Life Cycle
🕳️
Supernova Remnants
Massive stars end in spectacular supernovae, scattering heavy elements across space. The Crab Nebula is the remnant of a supernova recorded by Chinese astronomers in 1054 AD.
Death & Rebirth

Four Pillars
of Nebular Form

Each class tells a different story of birth, transformation, and the relentless recycling of cosmic matter.

Emission
Gas clouds energised by nearby hot stars, glowing in brilliant reds and pinks from ionised hydrogen.
Reflection
Dust clouds that scatter and reflect nearby starlight, appearing in ghostly blue hues across the sky.
Dark
Opaque dust pillars that absorb all light, revealing themselves only as silhouettes against brighter backgrounds.
Planetary & Supernova
Stellar remnants — the beautiful end-stages of stellar evolution, from delicate planetary shells to explosive supernova shockwaves expanding at thousands of km/s.
